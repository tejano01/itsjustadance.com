Safety Wasn't an Afterthought

A stranger outside a bar asked a question I didn't have an answer to. Then I built one.

M
M. Rivera Founder, It's Just A Dance · June 25, 2026

It was a Friday night outside Whiskey River North. I was showing the app to a small group of women — doing what I've been doing most weekends, talking to strangers, watching their faces as they figure out what it is.

The reaction to the rating screen was the usual one. People light up. It's consistently the feature that lands best in every demo.

Then one of them asked:

"What if someone is too handsy? Can I make sure I never match with them again?"

She didn't say it with alarm. Just matter-of-fact. Like it was a reasonable thing to want.

I didn't have a great answer. The rating system existed. The "Not a great fit" option existed. But there was nothing that guaranteed you'd never see that person again. I told her I'd think about it.

I thought about it on the drive home. By the time I got there, I knew what it needed to be.

What I built.

When you rate a dance "Not a great fit," something new appears beneath the rating options. Small. Optional. No explanation required.

Always pass if we match again

One tap. Silent. Permanent. No notification to the other dancer. No confrontation. They simply never appear in your browse list again.

I spent a long time on the word "always." Not "block." Not "never show again." Always pass. Because it isn't a confrontation — it's a preference. The dance floor equivalent of quietly choosing a different corner of the room. And "always" does all the work. It's self-defining. You don't need a tooltip explaining what permanent means.

It ships without ceremony. No success toast. No confirmation dialog. Silence is the point.

The two features that were already there.

Mutual matching. Both dancers have to tap Ask on each other before any connection is revealed. Nobody can approach you through the app that you didn't already express interest in. No unsolicited contact. No stranger who found your profile. The floor stays yours. Most people think of mutual matching as the fun part — the moment both colors light up. It is. But it's also why nobody can use this app to corner you.

No photos. No messaging. I've written before about why there are no photos. But there's a safety dimension I didn't spell out: no photos means no collecting. No screenshotting profiles. No sending someone's image somewhere it was never meant to go. When the night ends, everything disappears. There's nothing to take with you except the memory of the dance.

How the app gets better.

I didn't design this app with a safety framework in mind. I designed it to solve a specific problem — the awkwardness of asking a stranger to dance. The safety properties emerged from that, mostly naturally, mostly because the same instincts that make rejection on a dance floor uncomfortable also make surveillance and pressure uncomfortable.

But the third feature — the one she asked for — that one came directly from her. A stranger outside a bar on a Friday night, matter-of-fact about a reasonable thing she wanted.

That's how the app gets better. Not from a roadmap. From real people asking real questions.

"One song. One moment. One dance."
